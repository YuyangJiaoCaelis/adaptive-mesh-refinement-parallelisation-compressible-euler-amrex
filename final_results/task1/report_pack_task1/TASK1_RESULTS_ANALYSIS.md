This section reports the one-dimensional Riemann tests from Toro (Table 4.1) that were used to validate the Euler solver and the AMR strategy. All five standard test problems were run with an ideal-gas equation of state with \(\gamma=1.4\), a single discontinuity at \(x_0=0.5\), and the standard final times used in the literature and in the code setup (test 1: \(t=0.25\), test 2: \(t=0.15\), test 3: \(t=0.012\), test 4: \(t=0.035\), test 5: \(t=0.035\)). The left and right states for each test follow Toro Table 4.1 and are used consistently in the exact-solution generation (see `postprocess_1d.py` in `NUMERICAL_METHOD_EVIDENCE.md`). The numerical method used for these runs is a second-order, Riemann-problem-based finite-volume scheme using HLL fluxes with limited linear reconstruction and a two-stage update; the run configuration explicitly enforces non-first-order mode via `adv.first_order=0` (see `NUMERICAL_METHOD_EVIDENCE.md` and the source pointers it contains).

The initial data for the five Toro tests are as follows. Test 1 uses \((\rho_L,u_L,p_L)=(1.0,0.0,1.0)\) and \((\rho_R,u_R,p_R)=(0.125,0.0,0.1)\). Test 2 uses \((\rho_L,u_L,p_L)=(1.0,-2.0,0.4)\) and \((\rho_R,u_R,p_R)=(1.0,2.0,0.4)\). Test 3 uses \((\rho_L,u_L,p_L)=(1.0,0.0,1000.0)\) and \((\rho_R,u_R,p_R)=(1.0,0.0,0.01)\). Test 4 uses \((\rho_L,u_L,p_L)=(1.0,0.0,0.01)\) and \((\rho_R,u_R,p_R)=(1.0,0.0,100.0)\). Test 5 uses \((\rho_L,u_L,p_L)=(5.99924,19.5975,460.894)\) and \((\rho_R,u_R,p_R)=(5.99242,-6.19633,46.0950)\). These states are the standard Toro Table 4.1 configurations and match the parameters used for the exact reference solutions in the post-processing.

Uniform-grid validation was performed with three resolutions, \(N=100\), \(200\), and \(400\) cells (no AMR). For each test, a single “uniform convergence” figure overlays \(\rho\), \(u\), \(p\), and specific internal energy \(e\) for all three resolutions, together with the exact Riemann solution. These plots are provided as `fig_toro1d_test1_uniform.pdf` through `fig_toro1d_test5_uniform.pdf` (with matching `.png` files), and are indexed in `FIGURE_TABLE_INDEX.md`. The qualitative behaviour matches the expected wave patterns for each problem class, including shock and contact discontinuities where present, and the higher-resolution curves track the exact solution more closely in the smooth regions between discontinuities.

Convergence is quantified using cell-width-weighted L1 errors computed between the sampled numerical solution and the exact solution on the same \(x\)-locations for \(\rho\), \(u\), \(p\), and \(e\). The complete error table and observed orders are provided in `toro1d_errors.md` with a machine-readable copy in `toro1d_errors.csv`. For the uniform runs, the density error decreases between \(N=100\) and \(N=400\) for every test, with L1(\(\rho\)) reducing from \(9.19\times10^{-3}\) to \(5.17\times10^{-3}\) in test 1, from \(2.01\times10^{-2}\) to \(1.30\times10^{-2}\) in test 2, from \(1.59\times10^{-1}\) to \(6.43\times10^{-2}\) in test 3, from \(1.67\times10^{-1}\) to \(6.09\times10^{-2}\) in test 4, and from \(7.00\times10^{-1}\) to \(3.72\times10^{-1}\) in test 5 (all values taken directly from `toro1d_errors.csv`). The observed orders in `toro1d_errors.md` vary by test and by variable. This is expected because these solutions contain discontinuities, and L1 convergence for shock-dominated flows is typically closer to first order even when a formally second-order scheme is used. In this context, the primary validation objective is that errors decrease overall with resolution and that the numerical profiles converge to the exact reference away from discontinuities, both of which are demonstrated by the figures and tables.

The same five tests were then re-run with AMR using the lowest uniform resolution as the base grid (base \(N=100\)) and refinement ratios of two between levels. The AMR cases were configured to reach effective resolutions comparable to \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=200\) (one refinement level) and \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\) (two refinement levels), and the same L1 error analysis was repeated (rows with `mode=amr` in `toro1d_errors.md` and `toro1d_errors.csv`). To satisfy the requirement that refinement focuses only on sharp features, each AMR-versus-uniform figure includes a visible representation of AMR patch placement as translucent level bands that do not obscure the underlying solution curves. These are the files `fig_toro1d_test1_amr_vs_uniform.pdf` through `fig_toro1d_test5_amr_vs_uniform.pdf` (with matching `.png` files), and they directly show that refined patches are concentrated near wave features.

The localisation of refinement is also quantified independently using AMReX box metadata, which is summarised in `amr_coverage_summary.md` at matched high effective resolution (\(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\)). For example, in test 1 the level-1 coverage is 16% of the domain and level-2 coverage is 8%, localised to narrow x-intervals around the discontinuities (L1: \([0.680,0.760]\) and \([0.900,0.980]\); L2: \([0.710,0.750]\) and \([0.920,0.960]\)). In test 4 the refined coverage is even smaller (L1: 14%, L2: 12%), with refined intervals concentrated around \(x\approx0.2\)–\(0.34\). In contrast, test 2 and test 5 display broader refinement, consistent with their stronger or more spatially extended gradients (test 2: L1 38% and L2 34%; test 5: L1 48% and L2 44%). These values demonstrate that smooth regions remain mostly coarse while refinement follows sharp features, as required.

At matched high effective resolution, AMR and uniform results are generally in the same accuracy regime, with problem-dependent differences that are captured in `toro1d_errors.md` and visible in the AMR-versus-uniform figures. In test 1, AMR improves the L1 errors at \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\) relative to the uniform \(N=400\) case, with L1(\(\rho\)) decreasing from \(5.17\times10^{-3}\) to \(2.61\times10^{-3}\) and L1(\(p\)) decreasing from \(4.63\times10^{-3}\) to \(1.63\times10^{-3}\). In test 5, AMR also produces substantially smaller L1 errors at \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\), for example reducing L1(\(p\)) from \(1.46\times10^{1}\) (uniform) to \(4.95\times10^{0}\) (AMR). In the strongest-shock test (test 3), the AMR and uniform errors at \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\) are close for density and velocity and modestly improved for pressure and internal energy (for instance, L1(\(p\)) is \(5.95\) for uniform and \(4.97\) for AMR). A small counter-example is test 4, where AMR is slightly worse than uniform at \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\) for some variables (e.g. L1(\(u\)) is \(5.41\times10^{-2}\) for uniform and \(7.08\times10^{-2}\) for AMR). This behaviour is consistent with a refinement strategy that targets the strongest gradients but does not necessarily guarantee improved global norms for every variable in every case, especially when the refined region is relatively small and the reference solution contains multiple interacting features.

Efficiency is evaluated with two complementary metrics derived from existing solver logs and reported in `task1_cost_metrics.md` (raw in `task1_cost_metrics.csv`). The first is wall-clock runtime (the solver’s reported total run time), and the second is an algorithmic work proxy given by the total number of “updated cells” accumulated from log lines of the form `Advanced ... cells` over all levels and timesteps. The updated-cell ratios show that AMR can reduce work when refinement remains localised. Averaged over all five tests, the mean updated-cell ratio (AMR/uniform) is 0.730 at \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=200\) and 0.948 at \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\), while the mean runtime ratios are 1.271 and 1.401 respectively (see the “Resolution-wise mean ratios” block in `task1_cost_metrics.md`). The mismatch between work reduction and runtime reduction is important and should be discussed explicitly: in these small 1D-strip runs the overheads of regridding, refluxing, and multi-level bookkeeping can dominate wall-clock time even when the total number of cell updates decreases. The per-test results illustrate this clearly. In test 3 at \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\), AMR reduces updated cells to 0.673 of the uniform total but still has a runtime ratio of 1.128, while test 2 shows the opposite pattern at \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=400\), where broad refinement (as indicated by `amr_coverage_summary.md`) increases updated cells to 1.380 of uniform and increases runtime to 1.829 of uniform. These outcomes are consistent with the observed coverage fractions: when refinement occupies a large fraction of the domain, AMR can approach or exceed the work of a uniform fine grid, while still paying the overhead costs.

Overall, the 1D Toro suite provides a complete validation dataset in the format required by the assignment. The uniform-grid figures and L1 tables demonstrate resolution-dependent error reduction and good agreement with exact Riemann solutions, and the AMR figures provide both accuracy comparisons and clear, non-obscuring visual evidence of patch placement. The combined use of `toro1d_errors.md`, `amr_coverage_summary.md`, and `task1_cost_metrics.md` supports a balanced discussion that separates accuracy, refinement localisation, and computational cost. This makes the conclusions robust: AMR does refine sharp features while leaving smooth regions coarse, and it can reduce the amount of solution work in a way that is measurable, but in very small problems wall-clock improvements are not guaranteed because AMR overhead can dominate.
